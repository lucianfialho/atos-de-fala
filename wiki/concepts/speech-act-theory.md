# Speech Act Theory

## Definition

The pragmatics tradition (Austin, *How to Do Things with Words*, 1962; Searle, *Speech Acts*,
1969) holding that utterances *do things* beyond stating truths. The core notion is
**illocutionary force**: the act a speaker performs (asserting, requesting, promising, thanking)
that cannot be read off syntax alone — "Você pode me passar o sal?" is grammatically a question
but pragmatically a request.

## How It Works

Austin split an utterance into locutionary (what is said), **illocutionary** (what is done), and
perlocutionary (the effect) acts. Searle systematized illocutionary acts into **5 macro-classes**:

- **Assertives** — commit the speaker to truth (afirmar, informar, concordar/discordar).
- **Directives** — get the hearer to act (pedir, perguntar, sugerir).
- **Commissives** — commit the speaker to future action (prometer, oferecer).
- **Expressives** — express a psychological state (agradecer, desculpar, saudar, despedir).
- **Declarations** — change reality by being said (declarar aberto, demitir) — rare in open text.

ISO 24617-2's general-purpose functions are an operationalization of this tradition for annotation.

## Why It Matters

This — not Chomsky's syntax — is the theoretical basis of the atos model. Our taxonomy maps
each act to a Searle class so the label set is principled, not ad hoc. Indirect speech acts
(common in PT-BR: "poderia...?") are why surface form alone is insufficient and a learned model
is justified.
